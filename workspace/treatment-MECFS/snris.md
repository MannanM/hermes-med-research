# SNRIs (Serotonin-Norepinephrine Reuptake Inhibitors) for ME/CFS

> Compiled: 2026-05-27
> **Disclaimer:** This is not medical advice. Consult a healthcare professional before trying any treatment.

---

## Overview

Serotonin-norepinephrine reuptake inhibitors (SNRIs) are a class of antidepressants that increase levels of both serotonin and norepinephrine in the brain by blocking their reabsorption. Common SNRIs include duloxetine (Cymbalta), venlafaxine (Effexor), and desvenlafaxine (Pristiq). In the context of ME/CFS, SNRIs are of interest because they can help with pain (particularly duloxetine, which is FDA-approved for fibromyalgia), mood, and potentially energy regulation through norepinephrine pathways. They are often preferred over SSRIs by ME/CFS specialists because they are less sedating and may offer broader symptom relief.

## Current Status

| Aspect | Status |
|--------|--------|
| Evidence level | **Low-Moderate** — used by specialists, supported by clinical experience and extrapolation from fibromyalgia trials |
| FDA approved for ME/CFS | ❌ No |
| Off-label use | Common — prescribed by ME/CFS specialists for pain and mood |
| Key risks | Nausea, dry mouth, insomnia, increased blood pressure; discontinuation syndrome with abrupt cessation |
| Community interest | Moderate — mixed experiences; some benefit for pain and mood without worsening fatigue |

## Scientific Findings

### Update — 2026-05-26
**Study:** *Underuse of Pharmacologic Therapies for Myalgic Encephalomyelitis/Chronic Fatigue Syndrome Before Specialist Evaluation* (PMID: [42055743](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42055743/))
**Local Summary:** [Layperson Summary](../articles-MECFS/2026-05-26/42055743-summary.md)

**Findings:**
- In a retrospective study of 571 patients referred to an ME/CFS specialty clinic (2018–2022), SNRIs were the most commonly prescribed medication class potentially intended for ME/CFS symptom management.
- The study found that while SNRIs were the most common pharmacotherapy tried by patients before specialist evaluation, overall use of ME/CFS-specific pharmacotherapy was still limited (68.3% of patients had tried at least one ME/CFS-relevant medication).
- SNRIs were used more often than other specialist-recommended classes like TCAs or gabapentin, suggesting they may be the most accessible pharmacotherapy option in primary care for ME/CFS.
- The authors note that better education of clinicians about available treatment options — including SNRIs — could improve management of ME/CFS.

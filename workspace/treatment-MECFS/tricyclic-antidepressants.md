# Tricyclic Antidepressants (TCAs) for ME/CFS

> Compiled: 2026-05-27
> **Disclaimer:** This is not medical advice. Consult a healthcare professional before trying any treatment.

---

## Overview

Tricyclic antidepressants (TCAs) are an older class of antidepressant medications that work by blocking the reuptake of serotonin and norepinephrine, though with broader receptor activity than newer antidepressants. Common TCAs include amitriptyline, nortriptyline, and desipramine. In ME/CFS management, TCAs — particularly amitriptyline at low doses (10–50 mg) — are frequently used off-label for their analgesic (pain-relieving), sleep-promoting, and mildly antidepressant effects. Their sedating properties can be both a benefit (for sleep disturbances) and a drawback (for daytime fatigue). TCAs have a long history of use in ME/CFS and fibromyalgia, predating the availability of SNRIs.

## Current Status

| Aspect | Status |
|--------|--------|
| Evidence level | **Low-Moderate** — clinical experience supports use for pain and sleep in ME/CFS; some small studies |
| FDA approved for ME/CFS | ❌ No |
| Off-label use | Common — especially low-dose amitriptyline for sleep and pain |
| Key risks | Sedation, dry mouth, constipation, weight gain, cardiac arrhythmias at higher doses; dangerous in overdose |
| Community interest | Moderate — well-established option but side effects limit tolerability |

## Scientific Findings

### Update — 2026-05-26
**Study:** *Underuse of Pharmacologic Therapies for Myalgic Encephalomyelitis/Chronic Fatigue Syndrome Before Specialist Evaluation* (PMID: [42055743](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42055743/))
**Local Summary:** [Layperson Summary](../articles-MECFS/2026-05-26/42055743-summary.md)

**Findings:**
- In a retrospective study of 571 patients referred to an ME/CFS specialty clinic, tricyclic antidepressants were the third most commonly identified medication class used for ME/CFS symptom management, after SNRIs and gabapentin.
- The study noted that with the exception of medications used for pain and anxiety (including TCAs), use of pharmacotherapy for ME/CFS symptom management as proposed by specialists was limited overall.
- This suggests TCAs occupy a specific niche in ME/CFS treatment — primarily for pain and sleep — but that other classes of recommended pharmacotherapy may be even more underutilized.
- Better clinician education about the full range of ME/CFS treatment options — not just pain-focused ones — could broaden access to more targeted therapies.

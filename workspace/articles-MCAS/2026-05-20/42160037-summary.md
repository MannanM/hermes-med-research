# Plain Language Summary: Objective Evaluation of the Effects of Ramadan Fasting on Nasal Respiratory Function

> **Original Study:** [PMID 42160037](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42160037/) | **Published:** 2026-05-20
> **Journal:** Journal of Craniofacial Surgery
> **Local Resources:** [Metadata](./42160037.md) | [Full Text](./42160037-full.txt)

## Overview

⚠️ **Note: This article is NOT about MCAS.** The acronym "MCA" in this paper stands for "minimal cross-sectional area" — a measurement of nasal airway patency — not mast cell activation syndrome. This was retrieved by the PubMed search due to an acronym overlap.

This prospective study examined whether Ramadan fasting (daytime abstinence from food and drink) affects nasal breathing function. Sixty-two healthy participants underwent acoustic rhinometry and rhinomanometry measurements at baseline, on the first day of fasting, and on the 30th day of fasting.

## Key Findings

- After 30 days of Ramadan fasting, participants showed significant improvements in nasal volume, airflow, and breathing comfort.
- Total airflow, total resistance, and nasal volume measurements all improved significantly from baseline to day 30.
- Participants reported better subjective breathing comfort.
- The study suggests fasting may have a positive effect on nasal respiratory function.

## What This Means for MCAS Patients

This study is not directly relevant to MCAS. However, some MCAS patients explore dietary modifications (including intermittent fasting) as part of their symptom management. Any potential effects of fasting on nasal or respiratory function should not be confused with therapeutic effects on mast cell activation.

## Investigated Treatments / Interventions

- Ramadan fasting (intermittent daytime fasting) — studied for effects on nasal respiratory function in healthy individuals, not MCAS.

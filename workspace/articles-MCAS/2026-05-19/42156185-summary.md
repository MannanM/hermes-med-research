# Plain Language Summary: The REMA Score Revisited — An Optimized Algorithm for Early Prediction of Clonal Mast Cell Disease

> **Original Study:** [PMID 42156185](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42156185/) | **Published:** 2026-05-19
> **Journal:** Allergy
> **Local Resources:** [Metadata](./42156185.md) | [Full Text](./42156185-full.txt)

---

## Overview

The REMA score is a diagnostic tool developed by the Spanish Network on Mastocytosis to help doctors identify patients who may have a clonal mast cell disorder — a condition where mast cells (immune cells that release histamine and other inflammatory mediators) are abnormal and prone to over-activation. This is particularly important for patients who experience severe allergic-type reactions (anaphylaxis) but do NOT have the skin symptoms typically associated with mastocytosis.

After 15 years of using this scoring system in clinical practice, researchers decided to take a fresh look at how well it actually works. They analyzed data from 1,204 patients who were referred to the Spanish mastocytosis network with severe mast-cell-related symptoms but no skin involvement. All patients underwent a thorough evaluation including allergy testing, blood tryptase measurements, bone marrow examination, and genetic testing.

## Key Findings

- **The REMA score catches most cases (87–90% sensitivity)** — it rarely misses a true clonal mast cell disorder, which is good for a screening tool.
- **But it also flags many people who turn out not to have the condition (only 34–35% specificity)** — meaning about two out of every three positive screens are false alarms, leading to unnecessary bone marrow biopsies.
- **64% of patients who underwent bone marrow examination were ultimately diagnosed with a clonal mast cell activation disorder** — confirming that this is a common underlying cause in patients with severe anaphylaxis without skin signs.
- **For patients with borderline scores (0–2 range),** the researchers identified two additional clues that help predict who truly needs a bone marrow biopsy:
  - Elevated blood tryptase levels (without hereditary alpha tryptasemia, a genetic condition that naturally elevates tryptase)
  - A known trigger of insect venom (bee/wasp stings)
- **KIT p.D816V mutation testing in blood** — a genetic marker specific to mast cell disorders — was helpful but only detected in 29% of confirmed cases.
- **The refined algorithm** improved accuracy, raising specificity to 57% while maintaining sensitivity at 86%.

## What This Means for MCAS Patients

This study is directly relevant to the MCAS community because it addresses one of the most challenging diagnostic dilemmas: distinguishing clonal mast cell disorders (like indolent systemic mastocytosis or monoclonal mast cell activation syndrome) from non-clonal MCAS. Both conditions can present identically with severe allergic-type reactions, but they have different treatment implications and long-term outlooks.

The refined REMA scoring algorithm gives doctors a better tool to decide which patients with severe anaphylaxis truly need an invasive bone marrow biopsy — reducing unnecessary procedures while still catching the cases that matter. For MCAS patients, this could mean more accurate diagnoses and fewer invasive tests.

However, it's important to note that this study focuses specifically on clonal mast cell disorders (where abnormal mast cells are driven by genetic mutations), not on the broader, more common form of MCAS where mast cells are hyper-reactive but not necessarily clonal. The distinction matters because treatment approaches differ.

## Investigated Treatments / Interventions

No specific treatments or therapeutic interventions were evaluated in this study. This is a **diagnostic accuracy study** focused on improving the identification of patients who need further testing (bone marrow biopsy). The interventions discussed are diagnostic procedures:
- REMA score calculation
- Serum tryptase measurement
- KIT p.D816V mutation testing in blood
- Bone marrow examination (histology, immunophenotyping)

# Plain Language Summary: Too High and Too Loose — Dysautonomia and the Hypertensive Paradox in Hypermobility Disorders

> **Original Study:** [PMID 42186391](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42186391/) | **Published:** 2026-05-21
> **Local Resources:** [Metadata](./42186391.md) | [Full Text](./42186391-full.txt)

---

## Overview
This review article tackles a confusing and under-recognized problem: high blood pressure (hypertension) in patients with hypermobile Ehlers-Danlos syndrome (hEDS) and hypermobility spectrum disorders (HSD). High blood pressure might seem like a separate issue, but it interacts dangerously with dysautonomia (autonomic nervous system dysfunction) — a condition affecting up to 70% of hEDS/HSD patients. The authors explain why standard blood pressure drugs may actually make these patients feel worse, and propose more targeted treatment approaches.

## Key Findings
- **Finding 1:** There are distinct "hypertensive endotypes" in hEDS/HSD: hyperadrenergic dysautonomia (overactive "fight or flight" system), paroxysmal hypertension (sudden blood pressure spikes), cervicogenic hypertension (linked to cervical spine instability), and nocturnal/supine hypertension (high blood pressure while lying down or sleeping).
- **Finding 2:** Standard blood pressure medications like beta-blockers and diuretics can paradoxically worsen symptoms by making orthostatic intolerance (dizziness upon standing) even worse, potentially increasing fall risk.
- **Finding 3:** Alpha-blockers and central sympatholytics (medications that calm the overactive sympathetic nervous system) show promise for the hyperadrenergic type of hypertension in this population.

## What This Means for Hypermobility Patients
If you have hEDS/HSD and high blood pressure, standard treatment guidelines may not work well for you. The key takeaway: blood pressure management in hypermobility disorders must account for dysautonomia. Ask your doctor about phenotype-directed therapy — specifically whether alpha-blockers or central sympatholytics might be more appropriate than first-line drugs like beta-blockers or diuretics.

## Investigated Treatments / Interventions
- **Alpha-blockers** (e.g., doxazosin, prazosin) — promising for hyperadrenergic phenotype
- **Central sympatholytics** (e.g., clonidine) — promising for hyperadrenergic phenotype
- **Withdrawal of provocative medications** — medications that worsen dysautonomia should be stopped
- **Avoidance of volume-depleting agents** (diuretics) — can worsen orthostatic intolerance
- **Phenotype-directed pharmacotherapy** — treatment matched to specific hypertensive subtype

# Plain Language Summary: 1st Tarsometatarsal Joint Instability in Hallux Valgus with Asymptomatic Pes Planus

> **Original Study:** [PMID 42185195](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42185195/) | **Published:** 2026-05-21
> **Local Resources:** [Metadata](./42185195.md) | [Full Text](./42185195-full.txt)

---

## Overview
This systematic review investigated whether instability of the first tarsometatarsal (TMT) joint — a joint in the midfoot — plays a role in the development or recurrence of hallux valgus (bunions) in people who also have flat feet (pes planus). The study analyzed 36 prior studies to determine if the first TMT joint is the primary driver of bunion deformity in flat feet.

## Key Findings
- **Finding 1:** First TMT joint instability is commonly found in people with both bunions and flat feet, but evidence suggests this instability is more often a result of the bunion deformity rather than its cause.
- **Finding 2:** In patients with mild, asymptomatic flat feet, bunion surgery (metatarsal osteotomy) generally does not lead to higher recurrence rates.
- **Finding 3:** The existing evidence does not justify routinely fusing the first TMT joint during bunion surgery in patients with mild to moderate flat feet — there are multiple potential points of instability along the foot, not just one.

## What This Means for Hypermobility Patients
Flat feet (pes planus) are extremely common in hypermobility/Ehlers-Danlos patients due to generalized ligamentous laxity. Bunions are also a frequent complaint. This study suggests that foot surgeons should not assume first TMT joint fusion is necessary in hypermobility patients with bunions and flat feet — the instability may be multi-factorial and more conservative approaches may be equally effective.

## Investigated Treatments / Interventions
- **Metatarsal osteotomy** — surgical realignment for hallux valgus
- **First TMT joint fusion** — surgical stabilization of the midfoot (not routinely recommended based on this evidence)

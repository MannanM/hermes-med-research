The association between hallux valgus and pes planus remains controversial with debate focussing largely on the postulated pivotal role of 1st tarsometatarsal joint. This is the first systematic review to evaluate the importance of 1st TMT joint instability in the development, or recurrence, of hallux valgus in asymptomatic pes planus. A systematic review of PubMed, Ovid EMBASE, Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL) & Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews (CDSR) and Wiley Online Library from inception to January 2026 was carried out. PRISMA guidelines were followed, the study registered with PROSPERO and the ROBINS-I criteria used to assess bias. Thirty-six studies met the inclusion criteria. Numerous large studies demonstrated an association between pes planus and the prevalence and severity of hallux valgus. Biomechanical, radiological and cadaveric studies showed increased 1stTMT joint mobility in hallux valgus in planus feet, but several series suggest that this may be a result rather than cause of the deformity while others have identified alternative points of 1st ray instability. Studies of surgical outcome showed mixed results, but mild, asymptomatic pes planus was generally not associated with clinically significant recurrence following metatarsal osteotomy, while more severe deformity was associated with modest increases in radiographic recurrence that appeared non-progressive. 1st TMT joint instability is common in hallux valgus with concomitant pes planus but is only one of many potential points of medial column failure and appears to be more often the result, rather than cause, of the deformity. The existing evidence does not justify routine 1st TMT joint fusion for hallux valgus in mild to moderate asymptomatic pes planus. level III.

# Plain Language Summary: Temporal Changes in Talofibular Distance on Ultrasound in Ankle Sprains

> **Original Study:** [PMID 42204070](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42204070/) | **Published:** 2026-05-27
> **Local Resources:** [Metadata](./42204070.md) | [Full Text](./42204070-full.txt)

---

## Overview
This study used 3-T MRI to determine the prevalence, size, and location of cartilage and osteochondral lesions in 171 athletes (166 athletes, 171 ankle injuries) with acute ligamentous ankle injuries. The study found that such lesions are more common than previously reported when using high-resolution imaging.

## Key Findings
- **Finding 1:** The prevalence of cartilage/osteochondral lesions (OCls) in acute ankle injuries was 14% — higher than the 8% reported using 1.5-T MRI.
- **Finding 2:** 85% of the lesions were cartilage injuries (not involving bone), and the majority were on the talus (ankle bone).
- **Finding 3:** Combined injuries to both the lateral ankle ligaments and syndesmosis ligaments showed the highest prevalence of OCls (23%).

## What This Means for Hypermobility Patients
Ankle sprains are extremely common in hypermobility patients due to ligamentous laxity and poor proprioception. This study shows that even acute ankle sprains frequently involve cartilage damage that may not be apparent on standard imaging. For hypermobility patients who experience recurrent ankle sprains, the cumulative cartilage damage could contribute to early ankle arthritis.

## Investigated Treatments / Interventions
None — this is a diagnostic imaging prevalence study.

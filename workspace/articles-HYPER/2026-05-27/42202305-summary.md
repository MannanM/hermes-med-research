# Plain Language Summary: Bony Procedures in Recurrent Patellar Instability

> **Original Study:** [PMID 42202305](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42202305/) | **Published:** 2026-05-27
> **Local Resources:** [Metadata](./42202305.md) | [Full Text](./42202305-full.txt)

---

## Overview
This review article discusses the role of adjunctive bony procedures — beyond standard ligament reconstruction — for treating recurrent patellar (kneecap) instability. While MPFL (medial patellofemoral ligament) reconstruction is the standard treatment, some patients have underlying bone abnormalities (malalignment, trochlear dysplasia, abnormal patellar height) that require additional corrections like tibial tubercle osteotomy, trochleoplasty, or femoral osteotomy.

## Key Findings
- **Finding 1:** MPFL reconstruction alone may not address all contributing factors in patellar instability — particularly in patients with bony abnormalities.
- **Finding 2:** Adjunctive bony procedures (tibial tubercle osteotomy, trochleoplasty, derotational distal femoral osteotomy) may be needed for patients with malalignment or dysplasia.
- **Finding 3:** A patient-specific approach integrating soft tissue and bony procedures is essential to reduce recurrence and preserve joint function.

## What This Means for Hypermobility Patients
Patellar instability (subluxation or dislocation of the kneecap) is extremely common in hypermobility/Ehlers-Danlos patients. While ligament reconstruction alone is often effective, hypermobility patients may also have underlying bony abnormalities that contribute to instability. This review highlights the importance of comprehensive evaluation (including assessment of alignment, patellar height, and trochlear anatomy) before surgical planning for patellar instability in hypermobility patients.

## Investigated Treatments / Interventions
- **MPFL reconstruction** — standard soft tissue stabilization
- **Tibial tubercle osteotomy** — realignment procedure
- **Trochleoplasty** — reshaping the femoral groove
- **Derotational distal femoral osteotomy** — correcting rotational malalignment

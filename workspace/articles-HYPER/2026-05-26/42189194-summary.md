# Plain Language Summary: Revision Surgery for Hip Endoprosthesis Inlay/Head Failure

> **Original Study:** [PMID 42189194](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42189194/) | **Published:** 2026-05-26
> **Local Resources:** [Metadata](./42189194.md) | [Full Text](./42189194-full.txt)

---

## Overview
This German-language technical article describes a surgical technique for minimally invasive revision of hip replacements when only the modular components (the plastic inlay or the ball head) have worn out or failed, while the metal cup and stem remain well-fixed. The technique involves cementing a new polyethylene inlay or cup into the existing metal shell, avoiding the need for a full revision surgery.

## Key Findings
- **Finding 1:** When the metal cup is well-integrated, isolated replacement of the inlay and/or head can avoid major revision surgery — particularly valuable for elderly or medically frail patients.
- **Finding 2:** The inner surface of the metal cup must be roughened for adequate cement bonding when using a non-matching inlay.
- **Finding 3:** Contraindications include infection, bone tumors, periprosthetic fractures, cup/stem loosening, and uncontrollable instability.

## What This Means for Hypermobility Patients
Hypermobility patients who undergo hip replacement at a younger age may eventually need revision surgery. The "isolated inlay/head exchange" technique described here is relevant when only the bearing surface has worn out and the rest of the implant is stable. However, instability is listed as a contraindication — and hypermobility patients are at higher risk for post-arthroplasty instability, which may limit the applicability of this technique.

## Investigated Treatments / Interventions
- **Isolated inlay/head exchange** — cementing new polyethylene inlay into existing metal cup
- **Revision head systems with metal sleeves** — to prevent ceramic head fracture

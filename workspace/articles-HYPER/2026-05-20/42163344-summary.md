# Plain Language Summary: REAC-Based Bioelectrical Neuromodulation for Canine Cranial Cruciate Ligament Rupture

> **Original Study:** [PMID 42163344](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42163344/) | **Published:** 2026-05-20
> **Local Resources:** [Metadata](./42163344.md) | [Full Text](./42163344-full.txt)

---

## Overview
This pilot study tested whether a non-invasive technology called REAC (Radio Electric Asymmetric Conveyer) — which delivers targeted bioelectrical stimulation — could help dogs with torn cranial cruciate ligaments (similar to ACL tears in humans) recover without surgery. Sixteen dogs with naturally occurring ligament ruptures were treated with a standardized REAC protocol and followed for 12 months.

## Key Findings
- **Finding 1:** All dogs weighing 15 kg (33 lbs) or less showed complete functional recovery after REAC treatment.
- **Finding 2:** Most dogs over 15 kg also showed complete or clinically meaningful improvement, though one large dog did not respond and required surgical referral.
- **Finding 3:** Improvements were durable — maintained at 12-month follow-up in all responder dogs.

## What This Means for Hypermobility Patients
This is a veterinary study, but the REAC technology is a non-invasive bioelectrical neuromodulation approach that could theoretically be applied to human connective tissue injuries. For hypermobility patients who frequently experience ligament sprains and tendon injuries, non-surgical treatments that support tissue healing are highly relevant. However, this is a small pilot study (16 dogs) and human trials would be needed before any conclusions can be drawn.

## Investigated Treatments / Interventions
- **REAC technology** — Radio Electric Asymmetric Conveyer, a non-invasive bioelectrical modulation platform
- **Protocol:** Veterinary Neuro Postural Optimization (VNPO), Veterinary Neuro Psycho Physical Optimization (VNPPO), and Reparative Tissue Optimization (TO-RPR)

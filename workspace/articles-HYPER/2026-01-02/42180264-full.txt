We read with great interest the study by Novak et al., “Hypermobile Ehlers-Danlos Syndrome: Cerebrovascular, Autonomic and Neuropathic Features.”1 In their sample of 270 persons with hypermobile Ehlers-Danlos syndrome (h-EDS) made by geneticists, the authors found clinically significant abnormal Survey of Autonomic Symptoms (SAS) score in 98.1% of patients with h-EDS and 0% in healthy control group in all domains, including orthostatic, sudomotor, vasomotor, gastrointestinal, and urinary, with the most common symptom being lightheadedness. This subjective autonomic symptom assessment was corroborated by objective autonomic function test results, which showed autonomic failure in 90% of h-EDS patients and none in healthy controls, with all 3 domains (cardiovagal, adrenergic, and sudomotor) being impaired in h-EDS. A tilt table test revealed a greater decline in cerebral blood flow velocity in h-EDS compared to controls, as well as the presence of postural tachycardia and orthostatic hypotension in patients, but not healthy controls. Additionally, quantitative axon reflex test, epidermal nerve fiber density, sweat gland density, and Neuropathy Total Symptom Score-6 were abnormal in patients versus controls.1

We believe the study is of major importance to the field of autonomic disorders because it underscores the broad spectrum, extent, and severity of the autonomic nervous system function impairment in patients with h-EDS. While the authors did not include a sample of patients with hypermobility spectrum disorders (HSD)—a diagnostic label often used for patients with similar clinical features and signs who do not qualify for the diagnosis of h-EDS by the 2017 Ehlers-Danlos syndrome diagnostic criteria2—we believe that, similarly to h-EDS patients, people with HSD have significant autonomic dysfunction, which manifests with a wide range of autonomic symptoms such as fatigue, brain fog, temperature dysregulation, gastrointestinal dysfunction, genitourinary symptoms, and others. Although there are no specific FDA-approved therapies for postural orthostatic tachycardia syndrome (POTS) and orthostatic intolerance, treatment of autonomic dysfunction exists and can be effective in reducing the symptom burden and improving quality of life in patients with h-EDS and HSD.3 These include beta blockers, midodrine, fludrocortisone, pyridostigmine, ivabradine, stimulants, intermittent intravenous saline, and immunoglobulin, when necessary, in addition to nonpharmacologic therapies consisting of increased sodium chloride and fluid intake, compression garments, dietary changes, and an individualized exercise approach.3,4

H-EDS and HSD are among the most common comorbidities in patients with common autonomic disorders, affecting at least half of patients with POTS.5,6 To this end, we believe that every patient presenting with autonomic dysfunction should be screened for h-EDS and HSD with a Beighton score—an easy scale that can be performed by any health care practitioner during a physical examination.2 Recognizing h-EDS and HSD among patients with POTS and other common autonomic disorders and identifying and treating autonomic symptoms in patients with h-EDS and HSD should become the standard of care in clinical practice.

Finally, both patients with autonomic disorders and those with h-EDS and HSD have significant chronic fatigue, with most patients experiencing fatigue as one of the most disabling symptoms and at least 20% of patients qualifying for the diagnosis of myalgic encephalomyelitis/chronic fatigue syndrome (ME/CFS).5,7 Conversely, greater than 90% of patients with ME/CFS have autonomic dysfunction.8 Similarly, almost 70% of patients with Long COVID have autonomic dysfunction, with about a half qualifying for the diagnosis of ME/CFS.9,10 Importantly, joint hypermobility has been identified as a risk factor for developing Long COVID after SARS-CoV-2 infection.11 Taken together, these associations and overlapping symptomatology suggest that autonomic dysfunction (also known as dysautonomia) is likely the core mechanism of fatigue in general, whether it is associated with connective tissue disorders (h-EDS and HSD), post-acute infectious syndromes (Long COVID and ME/CFS), or other systemic diseases and conditions.12 Diagnosis and treatment of the autonomic dysfunction is imperative to reducing symptoms and improving functional impairment.

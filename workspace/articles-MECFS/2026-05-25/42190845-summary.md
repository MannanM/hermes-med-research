# Plain Language Summary: Shared Genetic Risk Between Functional Somatic Syndromes, Mental Health Disorders, and Immune-Mediated Diseases

> **Original Study:** [PMID 42190845](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42190845/) | **Published:** 2026-05-25
> **Local Resources:** [Metadata](./42190845.md) | [Full Text](./42190845-full.txt)

---

## Overview

Functional somatic syndromes — conditions like ME/CFS, fibromyalgia, and irritable bowel syndrome (IBS) — frequently occur together with anxiety and depression. They also tend to run in families alongside autoimmune and autoinflammatory diseases. But why? Is it because of shared genes, shared environment, or both? This massive study from Sweden aimed to find out by analyzing over 6 million twins, full siblings, and half-siblings born between 1945 and 2003.

Using Swedish national health registries, the researchers identified people diagnosed with ME/CFS, fibromyalgia, IBS, major depression, anxiety disorders, and immune-mediated diseases. By comparing how often these conditions co-occur in identical twins (who share nearly 100% of their genes) versus fraternal twins and siblings (who share about 50%), they could estimate how much of the overlap is due to shared genetics versus shared environment.

## Key Findings

- **Finding 1:** ME/CFS, fibromyalgia, IBS, depression, and anxiety are all moderately heritable — meaning genes play a role — with heritability estimates ranging from 15% to 44%. However, the largest contributor across all conditions was the "unique environment" (factors specific to each person, like infections, injuries, or life events), which explained 49–84% of the risk.
- **Finding 2:** Fibromyalgia showed the strongest genetic overlap with immune-mediated diseases (genetic correlation of 0.52), suggesting that about half of the genetic factors that increase risk for fibromyalgia also increase risk for autoimmune conditions.
- **Finding 3:** ME/CFS showed a more modest genetic correlation with immune-mediated diseases (0.19–0.29), meaning there is some shared genetic vulnerability — but less than for fibromyalgia.
- **Finding 4:** Anxiety disorders showed almost no genetic overlap with immune-mediated diseases (genetic correlation of 0.04), suggesting that if anxiety co-occurs with autoimmune disease, it's likely due to environmental factors rather than shared genes.

## What This Means for ME/CFS Patients

This study provides strong, large-scale evidence that ME/CFS shares at least some genetic roots with immune-mediated diseases. This bolsters the view that ME/CFS has an immune/autoimmune component — it's not "all in the head." The finding also helps explain why ME/CFS patients may have higher rates of other conditions like fibromyalgia and certain autoimmune disorders in their families. On the other hand, the finding that most of the risk comes from unique environmental factors (rather than genes or shared family environment) suggests that triggers like infections, toxins, or injuries may play a major role in who develops ME/CFS.

**Limitation worth noting:** The study used registry ICD codes rather than clinical diagnostic interviews, which may not capture all cases accurately, especially for ME/CFS which is frequently underdiagnosed.

## Investigated Treatments / Interventions

This study did not evaluate any treatments or interventions. It is a genetic epidemiology study focused on understanding disease overlap and shared risk factors.

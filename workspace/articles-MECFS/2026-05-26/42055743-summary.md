# Plain Language Summary: Underuse of Pharmacologic Therapies for Myalgic Encephalomyelitis/Chronic Fatigue Syndrome Before Specialist Evaluation

> **Original Study:** [PMID 42055743](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42055743/) | **Published:** 2026-05-26
> **Local Resources:** [Metadata](./42055743.md) | [Full Text](./42055743-full.txt)

---

## Overview

ME/CFS is a complex neurological disease that causes severe fatigue, crashes after physical or mental exertion (called post-exertional malaise), unrefreshing sleep, and problems with thinking ("brain fog") or standing upright. The condition has become much more common since the COVID-19 pandemic. While ME/CFS specialists have developed treatment approaches using medications to manage symptoms, it has not been clear whether general doctors — the kind most patients see first — are offering these same therapies.

Researchers at an ME/CFS specialty clinic in Minnesota reviewed the medical records of 571 patients referred to them between 2018 and 2022. They checked which medications and supplements patients had already tried by the time they saw the specialist. The goal was to understand whether useful medications are being overlooked or underprescribed before patients receive specialized care.

## Key Findings

- **Finding 1:** About 68% of patients had been prescribed at least one medication that could potentially help with ME/CFS symptoms, but the types of medications were limited. The most common were certain antidepressants (SNRIs like duloxetine, and tricyclic antidepressants like amitriptyline) and gabapentin (a nerve pain medication).
- **Finding 2:** A slightly higher percentage — 72% — had tried at least one dietary supplement, most commonly vitamin D, various forms of vitamin B, and fish oil. This suggests many patients turn to supplements, perhaps because helpful prescription medications are not being offered to them.
- **Finding 3:** With the exception of pain and anxiety medications, the researchers found that medications recommended by ME/CFS specialists were generally underused. This points to a gap between what specialists know works and what general practitioners prescribe.

## What This Means for ME/CFS Patients

This study confirms a frustrating reality for many patients: even though there are medications that ME/CFS specialists use successfully to manage symptoms like pain, sleep problems, and fatigue, these same medications are often not prescribed by primary care doctors. Patients may need to seek specialist referrals to access more targeted treatment. The findings also highlight the need for better education of general clinicians about available treatment options for ME/CFS.

This is a retrospective (looking back at past records) study from a single specialty clinic, so the results may not apply everywhere. But the large sample of 571 patients makes the findings meaningful.

## Investigated Treatments / Interventions

- Serotonin-Norepinephrine Reuptake Inhibitors (SNRIs) — e.g., duloxetine, venlafaxine
- Gabapentin — nerve pain medication
- Tricyclic Antidepressants (TCAs) — e.g., amitriptyline, nortriptyline
- Vitamin D — dietary supplement
- Vitamin B12 and B Complex — dietary supplement
- Fish Oil — dietary supplement

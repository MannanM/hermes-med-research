# Plain Language Summary: When Dryness Extends to the Brain — Brain-Related Non-Sicca Manifestations of Sjögren's Disease

> **Original Study:** [PMID 42194915](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42194915/) | **Published:** 2026-05-20
> **Local Resources:** [Metadata](./42194915.md) | [Full Text](./42194915-full.txt)

---

## Overview

Sjögren's disease is a chronic autoimmune disorder best known for causing severe dry eyes and dry mouth — the classic "sicca" symptoms. However, this review paper highlights that the disease's effects extend far beyond dryness. Many patients also experience brain-related and psychiatric symptoms that can be just as disabling as the physical ones. This study reviewed the existing scientific literature to map out the full range of these "neuropsychological" symptoms, understand what causes them, and consider how to treat them.

The researchers looked at published studies describing how Sjögren's disease affects the brain and nervous system. They focused on cognitive problems, mood disorders, sleep issues, fatigue, and sexual dysfunction — areas that often receive less attention than the classic dryness symptoms.

## Key Findings

- **Finding 1:** Cognitive impairment — often called "brain fog" — is common in Sjögren's disease, affecting memory, attention, and the ability to plan and organize (executive function). This closely mirrors the brain fog that ME/CFS patients experience.
- **Finding 2:** Depression and anxiety occur more frequently in Sjögren's patients than in the general population. These mood issues interact with fatigue and sleep problems, making each other worse in a vicious cycle.
- **Finding 3:** While vaginal dryness (a physical cause of sexual dysfunction) is well recognized, the psychological aspects — including reduced sexual desire — have received much less research attention and may be underdiagnosed.
- **Finding 4:** The underlying causes are likely complex and may involve immune system dysregulation, inflammatory molecules (cytokines), hormonal changes, damage to small blood vessels, and the psychological impact of living with a chronic illness.

## What This Means for ME/CFS Patients

This study is directly relevant to the ME/CFS community because Sjögren's disease and ME/CFS share many overlapping symptoms — particularly brain fog, fatigue, and sleep disturbances. Both conditions involve immune dysfunction and inflammation. For ME/CFS patients who also have Sjögren's (a known comorbidity), this review validates that their cognitive and mood symptoms are real, physiologically based, and deserve proper medical attention. It also highlights the importance of multidisciplinary care that addresses both the physical and neurological/psychiatric aspects of autoimmune disease.

> **Note:** This is a narrative review summarizing existing literature — it does not present new original research.

## Investigated Treatments / Interventions

No specific treatments were evaluated in this review. The authors recommend multidisciplinary management strategies that address both glandular (dryness) and neuropsychological (brain fog, mood, fatigue) symptoms.

## Overview

Low Dose Naltrexone (LDN) refers to the use of naltrexone — an opioid receptor antagonist — at doses of approximately 1–4.5mg (versus the standard 50mg dose for alcohol/opioid use disorder). At low doses, naltrexone is thought to have paradoxical anti-inflammatory and immunomodulatory effects via transient opioid receptor blockade leading to compensatory endorphin upregulation.

## LDN in ME/CFS Context

### Proposed Mechanisms

1. **Endorphin upregulation** — Brief opioid receptor blockade → compensatory increase in endogenous endorphins and enkephalins
2. **Anti-inflammatory** — Reduces microglial activation and pro-inflammatory cytokine production (TNF-α, IL-6)
3. **Immune modulation** — Modulates toll-like receptor 4 (TLR4) signaling
4. **Pain modulation** — May help with the widespread pain component of ME/CFS

### Evidence Base

- Several small open-label studies show benefit in ME/CFS and fibromyalgia
- A 2010 Stanford pilot study (NCT00511485) showed improvement in ME/CFS symptoms
- Systematic reviews note the evidence is **promising but limited** by small sample sizes and lack of large RCTs

### Typical Protocol

| Aspect | Typical |
|--------|---------|
| Starting dose | 0.5–1.0mg at bedtime |
| Target dose | 3.0–4.5mg |
| Titration | Increase by 0.5mg every 1-2 weeks |
| Timing | Taken at bedtime (some experience vivid dreams) |
| Compounding | Requires a compounding pharmacy (not available as standard dose) |

### Common Side Effects

- Vivid dreams / insomnia
- Headache
- GI upset
- Usually transient — resolve within 1-2 weeks
